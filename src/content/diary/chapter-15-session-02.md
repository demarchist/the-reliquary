## Summer and Signals

The afternoon heat radiated off the Bloodstone plaza as the group gathered outside the Cathedral of [[Lathander]], still processing the news. The [[Sunsword]] — their Sunsword, the relic they had carried out of [[Barovia]] and donated in good faith to the Church of the Morninglord in Neverwinter — was gone. Stolen by a Paladin of the very order that had housed it. The threads were already tangling.

Tark bent and picked up a crumpled flier from the cobblestones. He turned it over in his hands and handed it to [[Patches]] — who remained in her human guise, brown-haired and unremarkable, her Drow features tucked safely beneath Alter Self — and together they puzzled out the hand-lettered text. A Summer Tournament. Open competition. Prizes. Held on the plains outside the city.

Tark looked up with interest. He was always up for a challenge.

The others were less enthused. The afternoon was fading toward evening, the heat finally beginning to relent, and the more pampered members of the group had been thinking about baths and beds for the past hour. They still needed to locate [[Gaddock]]. They needed to find appropriate lodgings. They needed intelligence on the city's underworld — whether a former master vampire was leaving a trail of bodies, and where the stolen sword might have gone.

Both Patches and [[Cassian]] had been reading the streets as they walked. [[Helioglabulus]] was thick with Thieves' Cant — signs scratched into doorposts, chalk symbols on kerb-stones, particular arrangements of goods in street vendors' stalls. A language built for those who knew to look. Patches had noted the symbols clustering in the direction of one of the outer districts. A specific establishment. If anyone in this city knew about unexplained killings, it would be the people who tracked that sort of thing professionally.

It seemed as good a starting point as any. And perhaps they could get lodging recommendations while they were at it.

## The Pickpocket

They moved through the evening city in a loose formation — the kind of procession that drew second glances even in a place as well-armed as Helios. [[JmJm]] and [[Buumi]] had fallen into an animated conversation, the bard and the paladin comparing gear with the particular enthusiasm of men who enjoyed being admired. Cassian listened with polite attention. Tark ranged slightly ahead, reading the crowd.

The tap against JmJm's hip was barely perceptible. The departure of the young human man who had caused it was accomplished in the same instant, swallowed into the foot traffic with the practiced invisibility of street work.

Patches moved. The thief did not see her coming — one moment he was free and clear, the next something had reached into his coat and extracted JmJm's purse before he had taken three more steps. She returned the coins to the bard without breaking stride.

Cassian had seen the whole thing from a different angle. A precise extension of one foot was all it took. The pickpocket stumbled directly into the shallow drainage channel running alongside the paving stones and went down in a spectacular splash of grey water. He sat up sputtering. A few nearby pedestrians laughed. Cassian caught Patches' eye with a wink, and she returned the smallest possible smile.

Behind them, Tark had turned at the sound of the fall. He doubled back to help the poor man up, apparently oblivious to what had just happened.

## The Cart-Wright's Inn

The establishment Patches had marked was tucked into the outer ring of the city — a cramped, older district of workshops and working-class housing where the Bloodstone in the walls was less decorative and more structural. She found it by the marks: a particular symbol scratched near a cart-wright's sign, a chalk notation on the step. The building below the workshop was the kind of inn that did not advertise.

A very fat halfling occupied a stool beside the entrance, apparently doing nothing in particular except existing as a door. He looked the group over as they approached — the weapons, the gear, the general aura of people who had been in a great many fights and won them — and let out a long, appreciative whistle.

They filed past him into the common room.

Every head turned. The silence that fell was the particular silence of people deciding whether to reach for their weapons. A handful of men and women at rough-hewn tables, a low fire, cheap tallow candles, the smell of bad cooking and cheaper wine. These were street-level operators, and what had just walked through their door was not street-level anything.

The man behind the bar was a grisly Half-Elf — silver scruff above a scarred jaw, the kind of face that had stopped being surprised by things decades ago. He looked the group over with the same practiced appraisal as the halfling outside, though his conclusion was harder to read.

Patches stepped forward and opened a conversation in Thieves' Cant.

The bartender was civil about it. He would let them into the proper establishment, he said. He genuinely wanted to. But he thought they were in the wrong place. This was a street-level shop. The people in this room stole chickens and picked pockets. The group were dressed in magic items that would buy this building several times over. He was not saying he couldn't help. He was saying he wasn't sure his help was what they were looking for.

JmJm solved the problem of the bristling room in the way he solved most problems: he performed. The tension cracked and then broke entirely as the bard filled the small space with music and warmth, turning a room full of suspicious cutpurses into an audience. By the time the bartender's expression had shifted from careful to amused, people were clapping along.

He opened a hidden door behind the bar. Stairs descended.

Patches and Buumi were first down. While they waited on the landing, Buumi received a quiet tutorial on the finer points of Thief Etiquette: what you took, when you took it, what you put back, and why discretion was the only currency that actually held its value.

At the bar, Tark noticed the man sitting nearest to him. He had the specific stillness of someone who has decided that the safest thing to do is nothing. His knuckles were white around his mug.

Tark settled onto the stool beside him and asked for the man's recommendation on the drink. The man unclenched, very slightly.

They got talking. The man — relaxing by degrees as it became clear this enormous monk was simply interested in having a conversation — mentioned something he'd heard on the street. That someone in the city had been calling Tark a *Kryshchek*.

The bartender caught the word and snorted.

"Northern bumpkins' thing," he said, leaning on the bar. "Mountain spirit. Small, red-haired folk who come down out of the high passes when their curiosity gets the better of them. Farmers leave out offerings for them — good luck, ward off bad weather, that sort of business." He looked at Tark. "Red hair. Comes from the mountains. Shows up out of nowhere. The peasantry put two and two together."

Tark considered this. He had been mistaken for worse things.

He was still considering it when moonshine shots appeared in front of him.

## Below Stairs

The proper den was twice the size of the room above — which was to say, not very large at all. A handful of candles. A handful of regulars. A bard who had been playing to an uninterested room until JmJm came down the stairs and rendered him immediately and comprehensively obsolete. The bard set down his instrument. JmJm had been half a moment from asking Patches to silence him anyway.

Tark found a seat next to a Tabaxi — lean, watchful, the particular feline stillness of a creature that is always calculating the distance to the nearest exit. He sat, and the Tabaxi looked him over. Tark looked back. He noted, in particular, the teeth.

He asked, carefully, whether the Tabaxi had drained anyone of their blood recently.

"Almost certainly," she said, and flexed her claws.

Not a vampire, she clarified, when Tark continued to watch her with an expression of polite concern. She drained people of blood the ordinary way. Tark absorbed this information and, apparently satisfied, ordered them both a drink.

At the back of the room, a door had opened. Both Patches and Cassian had already noted the office — a narrow space behind a heavy door, the kind of room that existed in guild establishments for conducting business that did not happen in common rooms. The door had opened because the noise of the new arrivals had been worth investigating.

The woman who stood in the doorway was old. Short silver hair, cropped close. A massive tome tucked under one arm, held with the unconscious ease of someone who always had a massive tome tucked under one arm. Her eyes were the kind that counted exits first.

JmJm greeted her with a fistful of gold coins, hurled in her direction as a gesture of goodwill. The coins bounced off the tome. She blinked at him.

Then she looked at Patches. Then at Cassian. She made a small gesture: *come*.

The room returned to itself. People went back to their drinks. Behind them, the shots in front of Tark multiplied.

Fëanon, alone at a table upstairs, was having a better time than the ambience suggested.

The young rogue — Igo, he said his name was, and he said it with the particular brightness of someone who recognised an opportunity and intended to seize it — had come to ingratiate himself with the most obviously powerful group to walk through the door in recent memory. [[Fëanon Serinde]] recognised the calculation behind the charm and was entirely fine with it. Local eyes were worth cultivating.

Igo had things to say. The city was dominated by Warlocks and Paladins — the Crimson Dawn was everywhere, its crimson tabards as ubiquitous as the Bloodstone in the walls. Mages were rare. The [[Red Wizards of Thay]] had a presence, occupying rooms in one of the noble houses, and that was enough to make most of the city nervous whenever magic was visibly involved.

But there had been another thing. Two weeks ago, Igo said, leaning forward: a great golden orb had floated over the city like a second sun. The peasantry had called it a sign of Lathander. The object had descended to park itself alongside Huron Tower, and black-cloaked figures had flown down from it to the tower, spent a day, and then flown back up. The next morning the orb had gone.

Fëanon's expression did not change. He nodded and refilled Igo's cup.

Nobody else he had spoken to had one of those, Igo added. It had to be wizards. A thing like that — golden, like a great sun — who else?

As for unusual killings or supernatural activity: Igo had heard nothing. No bodies drained of blood, no mysterious deaths beyond the usual toll of a city this size. Nothing out of the ordinary.

Fëanon thanked him, slipped him a piece of gold, and asked to be kept informed. Igo took the coin, pocketed it with a practiced hand, and went back to nursing his drink with the quiet satisfaction of a young man who had just made a useful connection.

## Madame Yu

The office was small and occupied mostly by the desk, the tome, and the woman who owned both. Her name was Yu, and she had the demeanour of someone who had been running a small criminal enterprise for long enough that very little surprised her and almost nothing impressed her.

She looked at Patches. She looked at Cassian. She told them, without particular unkindness, that an establishment of this calibre was not appropriate for people of their evident calibre, and that she meant this as a compliment to both parties.

Patches agreed. Then she reached into her coat and produced the silver harp badge — the one given to her by Lady Aldencroft.

Yu looked at the badge. She sighed, the way someone sighs when the universe has confirmed a suspicion they had been hoping was wrong.

She answered the questions. No troubling and unknown killings in recent weeks. No bodies, no unusual incidents, nothing she had not already investigated and filed away. If something like that were happening in the city, she would know. It was her business to know.

Patches asked for a recommendation for more suitable lodgings.

Yu gave one without hesitation: the Rising Sun. Upper district, fine food, exceptional service, ask for Mister Luko. Her tone carried the practiced neutrality of someone directing a problem toward someone better equipped to handle it.

Cassian noted the far wall of the office as Yu spoke. The panelling was slightly wrong — a seam that did not belong, a hairline gap at floor level that suggested a door rather than a plank. Access to the sewers, almost certainly. Every guild office had one.

They took their leave. In the common room, Patches collected her friends from their various social situations. Tark was slightly pink from the moonshine. The Tabaxi watched them go with an expression of mild regret.

## Contact Points

Outside, the evening had settled fully over [[Helioglabulus]]. The magical lamps of the city were flickering to life, casting warm amber pools across the Bloodstone pavement. The summer crowds were thicker than ever — not the purposeful traffic of the market but something more leisurely, the particular nighttime bloom of a warm city that had remembered how to enjoy itself.

Fëanon came down the stairs and rejoined the group, and they put together what they had learned. The Rising Sun was, by the architecture of what Yu had told them, a Thieves' Guild establishment — more precisely, a much larger and more powerful operation that ran through the respectability of an upmarket venue. Mister Luko was further up the chain than Yu. That was fine. They could work with further up the chain.

They got directions and headed out.

Fëanon lagged slightly behind. He had something he wanted to do, and he wanted an excuse to stop moving. He found a small cafe just off the main route — tables on the pavement, coffee, and the kind of clientele who came to cafes in the evening to be seen quietly enjoying themselves — and told the others to go ahead, that he would catch up in an hour.

He ordered coffee. He took out his spellbook. He began to work.

The spell was Instant Summons. Complex, exacting work — the kind that required the full attention he had not been able to spare since they arrived in the city. If he bound the book correctly, he would be able to call it back to his hand from anywhere, regardless of where it had been locked or stored. This was a precaution. He had certain suspicions about what the next few days might involve.

He was on his second cup and most of the way through the transcription when the commotion registered at the edge of his attention. Something further down the street — raised voices, someone on the ground. Fëanon did not look up. City noise was city noise.

Two Crimson Dawn Knights walked past his table in the direction of the disturbance: a Paladin and a Warlock, tabards crisp even at this hour. He watched them go.

He finished the spell. Closed the book. Paid for the coffee.

Then he went to see what had happened.

The manservant was well-dressed — or had been. Now he was sitting on the edge of a decorative garden plinth, held upright by the attention of the Paladin, his face emptied of everything except a white and rising panic. He could not say where he was. He could not say who he served. He could not say what he had been doing five minutes ago or five days ago. He knew his name in the way a man knows a word he has heard so often it has lost meaning.

The Warlock — she gave her name as Krinn — looked at Fëanon with professional scepticism when he offered his services. The Paladin introduced himself as Vance and was more willing to accept help from any direction.

Fëanon calmed the man with a soft word of magical suggestion and then turned his attention inward, threading his thoughts into the space behind the manservant's eyes.

What he found was very clean. Almost surgical. The memories were not scrambled or damaged — they were simply gone, lifted out with the precision of a tool designed for exactly this purpose. He had seen this before, in different contexts. He had spent time in the nautiloid ship beneath Ladypeak. He had read enough Illithid theory to understand the vocabulary of what he was looking at.

But that was not what had been done here.

He found the contact points. Ten of them, arranged in a pattern he recognised: the spread of an open hand pressed to either side of a skull. Fingerprints, essentially. Someone had cupped this man's head in their hands and simply taken everything he had.

Fëanon withdrew and straightened.

He told the Knights what he had found: the man was a victim, not a madman. Something had eaten his memories. The marks were deliberate and precise. He would recover physically, but what had been taken was gone.

Vance and Krinn exchanged a look. They thanked him — Vance with warmth, Krinn with the grudging acknowledgement of someone who did not like being beholden to an unknown wizard. They would take the man to the cloister of the Cathedral. There were healing resources there for exactly this kind of case.

Fëanon watched them go. He stayed on the pavement for a moment, turning what he had seen over in his mind.

No drained bodies. No puncture wounds. But in Al Askar, there had been another kind of vampire entirely — one that fed not on blood but on something harder to see and harder to track. An Emotional Vampire. He had fought that kind. He knew what their work looked like, and this was close enough to warrant attention.

He filed it away, crossed the street, and headed for the Rising Sun.

## The Rising Sun

The building occupied most of its block. Dark hardwood and richly painted stucco above a foundation of solid Bloodstone — the real structural kind, the heavy variety, which meant whoever had built this had been making a statement about permanence. Several turrets rose above the roofline, their teardrop peaks gleaming copper that had not yet had time to turn. Stained glass caught the lamplight from within, warm and concealing, shapes moving behind it but nothing legible.

The brass front doors were inlaid with panels. Workers, farmers, miners — the hands that built the city, rendered in exquisite detail. Patches and Buumi went in first to assess the situation.

The foyer was small and appointed in the same dark hardwood as the exterior, with red silk upholstery on the furnishings and a large man in exceptional clothing lounging against one wall. His coat was long silk, dark, trimmed in fur. His silk sash was colorful. His boots had a curled toe. His hands were jewelled. Two other men flanked the space in variations on the same dress: shaved heads, a bounce of curls at the crown, magnificent curled moustaches. One had muttonchops. These were people who took professional presentation seriously.

The coatman behind the golden-barred windows across the foyer greeted them with practiced warmth and looked them over with a practiced eye.

Patches named Mister Luko. The coatman welcomed them to the House — and explained, with complete pleasantness, that entry to the establishment required both disarmament and appropriate dress. Hospitality was guaranteed. Both were non-negotiable. He accepted their gear into magical lockup and handed each of them a slate and gold keycard.

The rest of the group arrived in stages. JmJm came in disguised as a dirty old beggar, poked his head around the door, then swept off the disguise in a theatrical flourish. Tark arrived in his gi. Fëanon appeared in his Netherese Arcanist's robes, prestidigitating himself clean and properly perfumed as he crossed the threshold.

Then the alarm went off.

A soft tone. Magical. The coatman looked at the board behind his bars with the expression of a man who had seen this before. He looked at Fëanon. He looked at the spellbook under Fëanon's arm. He explained, again with complete pleasantness, that the establishment could not admit an armed wizard.

The spellbook was a weapon, and the alarm had said so.

Fëanon stood in the foyer and underwent a private crisis. He had just spent an hour binding that book to his soul. He had carried it across planes. He had fought with it, slept with it, used it as a shield in extremis. The idea of leaving it in a locked box in a casino foyer under the care of a man with a curled moustache was not one his mind accepted easily.

The coatman waited with infinite patience.

Fëanon left. He told them he would return in an hour. He went back across the street to the same cafe, sat down at the same table, ordered more coffee, and stared at his book.

In the foyer, Tark was provided with a small set of noble clothes in orange silk, cut for a young human and therefore somewhat abbreviated on his frame. He wore them over the gi and achieved a look that could charitably be described as distinctive. Patches, in her noble wrap and boots, was awarded a lily corsage at the coat-check window — a small signal, visible to those who knew the language, that she was a VIP guest.

The bouncers opened the plush red leather doors.

## The House

The establishment's main floor hummed with the comfortable noise of money at rest. Sweet smoke drifted in haze above the tables. Silk-wrapped servers moved between gambling stations with silver trays. Booths lined the walls above the floor level, giving their occupants a view of the action. A raised walkway ran straight back through the tables to a grand staircase ascending to a second level, and beyond that a third — a large dining room visible through its floor-to-ceiling windows, candlelit and busy.

They found an opening at one of the gambling tables and took it.

An hour passed pleasantly. The drinks were excellent. The dice were friendly. When Fëanon eventually appeared — spellbook absent, dressed appropriately, somewhat over-perfumed — he found his companions in good spirits and over a thousand gold ahead of the house. He accepted a glass from a passing server and did not mention the memory-drained man in the decorative garden. Not here. Not in this room, with all these ears.

Three men in dark silks came down from the third level with the measured unhurried pace of people who work for someone important. They stopped at Patches' elbow and bowed.

*Mister Luko will see you now, M'lady.*

The walk up the stairs took them past the second floor — private rooms visible along the hall, the sounds of cards and conversation leaking under their doors — and up to the third, where the dining room presented itself in all its warmth and noise. Thirty seats, most of them occupied. Laughter. Music. A Goliath in the corner working through a leg of mutton with the cheerful singlemindedness of someone who had not been to a fine establishment in some time and intended to make the most of it.

At the head of the table sat a smaller man — older, late fifties, the kind of lean that came from never having been idle. Salt-and-pepper hair, neat silver goatee, skin that had paled gracefully with age. Bright almond eyes that had already completed their assessment of the group by the time they crossed the room. A coat of brilliant red silk, white ermine at the collar and cuffs. A jewelled ceremonial dagger in his black silk sash. Silver shoes with a curling toe.

He rose fractionally and gestured to the cleared seats at his left.

*Good evening. I am Mister Luko. You must be Lady Nora.*

Food arrived. Drink arrived. Luko was not the kind of man who rushed things he did not need to rush, and he did not need to rush this.

He had looked at the group when they came through his door and known what they were. They had come to ask him questions. He was in the business of information, and information had a price. He was, he made clear pleasantly, a reasonable man about pricing.

There had been no drained bodies in the city. No unexplained puncture wounds, no supernatural predators leaving evidence behind. Occasional incidents, yes — the city had its share of those, and the local adventuring groups handled them. But the specific kind of trail a vampire left: absent.

People went missing, of course. That was simply the arithmetic of a city this size. Speaking of which — he had recently put together a team for a specific job, and no one had returned. No bodies, no word, no interaction with the city guard or the Crimson Dawn. A team simply ceased to exist. The job, consequently, remained undone. His reputation, consequently, was suffering.

The job: steal [[House Muskov]]'s Seal. A direct commission. His previous team had gone in and vanished. He wanted the mystery solved and the job completed. He looked at the group over his wine glass. He had taken one look at them coming up his stairs and known.

In exchange: everything he knew about the current situation in the city. The location of Lord Aimu. And, potentially, a lead on the [[Sunsword]]. Plus ten thousand gold.

Alternatively — he had noted the slight stiffening at the table, the particular quality of attention that suggested some of his guests found the transactional framing ungracious — he would accept a powerful magic item in trade for the information alone.

JmJm looked at the people he had come here with. He looked at Luko. He looked at the jewelled dagger in the silk sash and the precise calculation behind the almond eyes. He reached, slowly, for the mysterious glove at his side.

Patches stood up. She extended her hand across the table.

*We'll take the job.*

Luko rose and clasped her hand with the smooth warmth of a man who had known she would say yes before she did. The deal settled into the room with a soft inevitability, like a key finding a lock.

JmJm looked at his friends for support.

His friends were watching Patches shake hands with a criminal boss.
