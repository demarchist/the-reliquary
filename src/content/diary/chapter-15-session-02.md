## Street Level

[[tarkathee-daru|Tark]] could make out enough of the flier to get the shape of it, with [[patches|Patches]] supplying the words he missed: a Summer Tournament, competitions scheduled for the fields outside the city walls, open to challengers. He expressed his interest. The rest of the group expressed their interest in lodgings and a lead on [[gaddock|Gaddock]], in roughly that order.

As they walked, [[jack-mayer-johnson-mathews|JmJm]] and [[horatio-von-bumidere|Buumi]] were demonstrating the finer points of their equipment to [[cassian|Cassian]], who listened with the patient attention of someone conducting their own quiet assessment. The display was attracting the wrong kind of attention. A pickpocket materialized from the crowd, made the practiced brush-and-lift against JmJm's side, and palmed his coin purse.

Patches had it out of the man's pocket before he had taken three steps, returned it to JmJm without a word, and kept walking. Cassian put his boot across the thief's path; the man went face-first into a puddle. Cassian caught Patches's eye and offered a small, unhurried wink. Tark went to help the man up.

Patches had been reading Thieves' Cant marks since they entered the city — chalk scratches on doorposts, worn notches in stone, small arrangements that meant nothing to anyone who hadn't been trained to read them. She had noted a particular establishment on the way through and turned them toward it now. They found it in a quiet outer district: a small inn pressed beneath a cart-wright's workshop, distinguished from the surrounding buildings by very little, except for a very overweight halfling balanced on a stool outside the entrance.

When the halfling took in their armor and weapons, he let out a long, low whistle. It was not appreciation.

Inside, a scarred half-elf with silver scruff stood behind the bar and watched them enter with the expression of a man calculating odds. Conversation in the common room stopped completely. The stillness that gripped the room was not the readiness of fighters — it was the held-breath quiet of people hoping not to be noticed by something that could kill them.

Patches spoke to the barman. He would show them into the proper establishment — the one below — but he wanted them to understand, with the strained politeness of a man choosing his words carefully, that they were not the usual clientele. Too well-equipped. Too obviously powerful. This was a street-level shop. JmJm performed. The tension dissolved. The barman lifted a section of floorboard.

## Old Lady Yu

[[feanon-serinde|Fëanon]] stayed upstairs and found himself a table with a young rogue called Igo, who could not pass up the chance to ingratiate himself with someone so clearly formidable and unusual. Fëanon saw the value of a pair of local eyes and bought the conversation with a frank promise of gold.

Igo knew the city. Beyond the Thayans — who had taken rooms in a noble house and were doing what Thayans did — there had been one other concentration of magical power in a city otherwise dominated by Warlocks and Paladins. Two weeks prior, a great golden orb had floated over [[Helios]] like a second sun and parked itself near Huron Tower. Black-cloaked figures had descended, spent the night in the tower, and returned to it the following morning before the whole enormous thing departed. The common people had taken it as a sign from the Morninglord. Fëanon filed it away carefully, pressed a handful of gold on the boy, and asked him to pass word if he heard anything strange.

Below, the underground establishment was roughly twice the size of the room above — another low-ceilinged space of rough tables and cautious-looking people. The local bard was playing when JmJm entered. He stopped, looked at JmJm, reconsidered his career, and set down his instrument. This was a mercy. JmJm had been moments away from asking Patches to silence him.

Tark sat down beside a Tabaxi — lean, yellow-eyed, with particularly pronounced fangs — who had been watching him with frank feline interest. He asked her directly whether she had ever drained anyone of blood. She replied, with equal directness, *almost certainly.* He asked if she was a Vampire. She laughed: no. She just drained people of blood the ordinary way, and extended one claw by way of demonstration.

At the bar, Tark asked the barman about the name people had been calling him in the street. A Kryshchek. The barman chuckled and set him straight: a northern folktale, he said — small, red-haired mountain spirits who came down into civilization out of curiosity. Locals left offerings for good luck. Evidently the market crowds had decided a small, red-haired monk wandering through a foreign city was close enough.

Tark absorbed this.

Meanwhile, Patches — wearing a human face, introducing herself as Nora — and Cassian had been shown into the back office, summoned by Old Lady Yu, who had appeared in the office doorway holding a massive tome under one arm: a compact woman with short silver hair and eyes that catalogued everything they rested on without apparent effort. JmJm had thrown a fistful of coins at her by way of greeting. They had bounced off the tome. She had gestured Patches and Cassian toward the office and shut the door.

Yu made clear, after the briefest exchange of pleasantries, that this was a lowly guildhouse and someone of Patches's obvious caliber had no business being here. Patches agreed and produced the Harper badge she had received from Lady Aldencroft. Yu looked at the silver harp badge and sighed with the weariness of someone whose afternoon had taken an unwelcome turn.

She answered the questions. No unusual killings. No bodies drained of blood. Nothing beyond the ordinary misfortune of city life.

And then — since Patches was clearly being shuffled upward — she gave a recommendation: Rising Sun, in the upper rings of the city. Exquisite lodgings, exquisite food, all the right people. Ask for Mister Luko.

Patches caught the implication clearly enough: she was being passed to a larger operation than this one. She noted a concealed exit at the back of the office — almost certainly into the sewers — and they took their leave.

## The Memory Thief

The evening had come on while they were underground, and the summer nightlife had transformed the streets: busier than the afternoon if anything, the crowds moving with the leisurely pace of people out because they chose to be. The scarlet tabards of the [[Crimson Dawn Knights]] moved through it all at steady intervals. The group collected Fëanon and set out across the city toward the upper rings.

The streets grew quieter and finer as they moved inward. Rising Sun occupied one of the largest buildings in the second-to-last ring — a multistory affair of dark hardwood and richly painted stucco over a foundation of deep Bloodstone, its teardrop-roofed turrets capped in copper still bright enough to catch the last of the evening sky. The stained glass windows glowed from within, warm and occluded, revealing nothing but movement. The front doors were brass, covered in paneled relief: workers, farmers, and miners building the city, posed with the deliberate dignity of people who knew they were being commemorated. It said money. It also said, to anyone paying attention, that it knew exactly what it was and saw no reason to be modest about it.

The foyer was a study in controlled impression. Dark hardwood paneling, red silk upholstery, small tables bearing objects placed purely to confirm that cost was not a consideration. Against one wall, a massive human man occupied a smoking chair with the comfortable authority of someone who had selected the best seat and intended to keep it — a long coat of rich dark silk trimmed in fur, fastened with a vivid sash, tight black silk trousers, boots of shining dark leather with a curling toe, hands covered in silver rings. Two more men stood near the coat-check windows, dressed identically, distinguished only by the color of their sashes. All three shared the same haircut: shaved clean to the scalp save for a tufty bounce of curls at the crown, and a moustache of magnificent size and considerable curvature. They watched the group arrive with professional composure.

The coat-check ran behind a row of golden bars, like a particularly elegant bank. The Coatman explained the terms: to enter the establishment proper, guests surrendered their arms and dressed accordingly. Hospitality was guaranteed. The guarantee went both ways. Magical lockboxes received their gear. Slate-and-gold keycards were issued in return.

[[jack-mayer-johnson-mathews|JmJm]] had arrived in the guise of a filthy old beggar and waited until a bouncer crossed the room to remove him before dropping the disguise entirely, spreading his arms wide and making a production of it. [[tarkathee-daru|Tark]] arrived in his Gi, [[cassian|Cassian]] turned out in tails and a top hat. [[gundren-rockseeker|Gundren]] and [[cirrus-al-kayan|Cirrus]] elected to remain outside. [[patches|Patches]] was given a lily corsage, pinned to her vest, and marked as a VIP without further ceremony. Tark was furnished with a noble's outfit sized for a young human, in orange, and put it on over the Gi without visible opinion on the matter.

Then [[feanon-serinde|Fëanon]] crossed the threshold with the [[iron-tome|Iron Tome]] under his arm, and something in the establishment's wards stirred. The Coatman informed him, with the practiced courtesy of someone who had delivered this news before, that a wizard so prepared could not enter the establishment. The spellbook would need to stay at the coat-check.

Fëanon considered the room.

He is, by any honest measure, one of the most dangerous mental presences alive — a master Enchanter who can unmake a man's memories, arrest a will in mid-thought, or bend a conviction so cleanly that the victim never knows the hand was on the wheel. A foyer full of bouncers, however impressively dressed, would be a manageable problem with the right preparation.

The right preparation was not what he had arrived with. Enchantment magic requires planning — spells loaded in advance, each casting shaped before the day begins. What he had available was everything else: fire, lightning, force, the blunt and spectacular arsenal of a wizard who had not been thinking about this particular obstacle when he woke that morning. He looked at the bouncers. He thought about what a fireball in an upscale nightclub would do to the group's standing in [[Helios]]. He thought about the Iron Tome — a legendary artifact of incalculable value — sitting in a coat-check box he did not control.

He argued briefly with the Coatman. The Coatman was unmoved.

Fëanon accepted the keycard for the Tome with the expression of a man recalculating, told the group he would return in an hour, and left.

Across the street, a small cafe had tables outside. Fëanon sat, ordered coffee, and opened the Iron Tome. He had two tasks: to load his enchantment magic properly, and to inscribe a specific working into the Tome itself — an Instant Summons, which would let him call the book to his hand from anywhere, at will. He could hand the Tome over at the coat-check and have it back before anyone noticed it was gone. The cost was the hour's preparation he hadn't thought to do before arriving.

A noblewoman at a nearby table had been giving him appraising looks for the better part of thirty minutes. He did not notice.

A commotion further down the street resolved itself, as he was finishing his work, into something specific: a well-dressed manservant in the grip of what appeared to be a complete mental collapse, standing near the edge of one of the small decorative gardens while two [[Crimson Dawn Knights]] tried to calm him. Fëanon — an expert on psionics, on Illithids, on the particular damage inflicted by things that fed on minds — paid his bill and walked over.

He quieted the man with a soft suggestion and examined what remained. The damage was immediately recognizable to someone who knew what to look for: not madness, not stroke, not any ordinary trauma. Something had eaten the man's memories. All of them. What was left was a person-shaped absence, panic filling the space where a life had been. He looked closer and found, pressed faintly into the skin around the man's skull, ten distinct contact points.

Fingerprints.

He told the knights — Vance and Krinn — that the man was a victim, that something had attacked him, that they should get him to the cathedral's healers. They thanked him and took the manservant away. Fëanon stood in the dusk-lit street and thought about Al Askar, and about the different varieties of predator that called themselves vampires — and about the fact that no blood-drained bodies had turned up in this city, not one, and yet here was a man scraped hollow by something that worked entirely differently.

He turned back toward Rising Sun and took stock of the foyer through the doors. His enchantment magic was ready now — precise, loaded, exactly what the situation called for. He counted the bouncers. He estimated. He ran the numbers on what he could hold simultaneously and what he could not, and on the consequences of one of them breaking free at the wrong moment and raising the alarm.

It was not quite enough to guarantee a clean result.

He handed the Iron Tome to the Coatman, prestidigitated himself clean and fragrant, and went through the doors with the particular composure of a man who has decided, privately, that this conversation is not over.

## Rising Sun

The others had been at it for an hour. Fëanon found them at a gambling table somewhere in the middle of the floor — [[patches|Patches]] with a precise, attentive look that belonged at a card table about as much as it belonged anywhere; [[tarkathee-daru|Tark]] winning with the honest directness he applied to most things and more often than the games' mathematics should have allowed; [[jack-mayer-johnson-mathews|JmJm]] talking to everyone within range and persuading three separate parties to make choices they would later reflect on; [[horatio-von-bumidere|Buumi]] delighted by everything, betting loudly and often, not especially concerned about the outcome either way.

A haze of sweet smoke hung over the floor. Silk-wrapped servers moved between the tables carrying silver trays. The noise was managed and deliberate — the clink of glassware, the movement of chips, polite laughter held just below a certain volume, the low continuous murmur of people selecting their words with care. Above, two mezzanine levels held dining booths and curtained halls; above those, a third-floor dining room glowed warmly behind large windows, figures moving in unhurried conversation, a long way up and clearly intended to feel that way.

Fëanon settled into an empty seat, collected a drink from a passing tray, and played. He said nothing about the man in the garden, nothing about the Iron Tome, nothing about the enchantment magic sitting ready and unused in his mind. The dice ran well. By the time the hour was through the group had a pleasant settled buzz and winnings clearing a thousand gold, and the assembled gentility of [[Helios]]'s upper ring had decided they were, on balance, rather good company.

## Mister Luko

The third-floor dining room held perhaps thirty covers and used most of them. Conversation moved through it in overlapping currents, the sounds of a serious meal being taken at leisure. At one end of the long table, a Goliath in fighter's gear was dismantling a leg of mutton with an enthusiasm that sat at considerable distance from the surrounding decorum. At the other end, Mister Luko reclined.

He was older — late fifties, perhaps — with salt-and-pepper hair worn neatly, a silver goatee trimmed to a point, and the pale complexion of a man who conducted his best business after dark. He wore a bright red silk longcoat with white ermine trim, a black sash, and shoes of dark elegant leather with a curling toe. His hands carried more silver rings than were strictly necessary. He looked the group over as they filed in with the unhurried patience of someone who had made a great many assessments and rarely been wrong.

*"Good evening. I am Mr. Luko. You must be Lady Nora."*

Space was made at the nearest seats. Food arrived without being asked for.

Luko was forthcoming in the specific manner of a man who intends to be paid for it. No bodies drained of blood, he confirmed. No victims found with puncture wounds. Supernatural predators drifted through the city occasionally and the local adventuring companies handled them — nothing out of the ordinary. People went missing, of course. People always went missing.

As it happened, a team of his had recently gone missing too. He had sent them to acquire House Muskov's Seal. No one returned. No arrest record, no commotion, no word at all — they had simply ceased to be accounted for. The job remained unfinished, his reputation with it, and he wanted to know what had become of his people.

He had taken one look at the group, he said, and formed an opinion. In exchange for completing the job and accounting for his missing crew, he was prepared to share everything he knew about the city's current situation — including the whereabouts of Lord Aimu, and by extension the [[sunsword|Sunsword]] — along with ten thousand gold.

[[jack-mayer-johnson-mathews|JmJm]] reached very slowly for a worn glove tucked into his belt and began to draw it on, finger by finger, with the focused patience of a man arriving at a decision.

Luko's eyes moved to the glove. They moved back to JmJm's face.

He revised his offer: a powerful magic item in exchange for the information, if the gold was beside the point. Something truly exceptional. His tone did not change.

JmJm looked around the table for support.

Patches stood, extended her hand across the food, and shook Luko's hand.
