## Ladypeak

The journey through the Nether Mountains took five days. The Mindslayers set a punishing pace — Garm breaking trail through waist-deep snow, Halar carrying enough equipment for three men, Khelon navigating by memory and starlight. The group kept up, though Gundren's shorter legs required him to ride Gwen for the steeper ascents. Tark, predictably, treated the march as training and shadowstepped ahead to scout each ridge.

[[Ladypeak]] rose before them on the sixth morning — a jagged peak in the western range, its summit wreathed in cloud. The ruins of the Lady's Hand Monastery clung to a shelf of rock two-thirds up the face, little more than broken walls and fallen pillars half-buried in snow. From a distance, it looked abandoned. Up close, Khelon pointed out the subtle signs: no birds nested on the ruins. No snow had accumulated in certain crevices. The rock face near the monastery showed faint discolouration — organic residue leached from below.

"Beneath this," Khelon said, his hand flat against the stone, "is a ship the size of a fortress."

## The Fissure

Garm led them to a concealed entrance on the mountain's northern face — a fissure barely wide enough for Halar's shoulders, hidden behind a fall of loose scree. The air that breathed from the crack was warm and carried a faint coppery tang.

The group descended single file. Tark went first, his blindfold's truesight piercing the darkness. Patches followed, her footsteps silent. Fëanon came third, and as they passed below a certain depth, he stopped.

"I can feel it," he said. "The web. It is still there."

Khelon gripped his shoulder. "Begin."

Fëanon closed his eyes. When he opened them, something had changed — a subtle tension in his posture, a slight narrowing of focus, as though he were listening to a sound no one else could hear. The psychic camouflage settled over the group like an invisible cloak. To the Elder Brain's senses, they would read as background noise — stray thoughts, ambient psionic static, part of the mountain itself.

The fissure opened into a tunnel. The walls were no longer stone — they were organic. Ridged, warm, faintly luminous with a blue-green bioluminescence that pulsed in slow rhythms. The floor was uneven, almost muscular. They had entered the nautiloid.

## The Living Ship

The tunnels of the nautiloid were worse than the simulation had prepared them for. The organic walls breathed — expanding and contracting in slow cycles that made the corridors widen and narrow unpredictably. Bioluminescent fluid seeped from pores in the ceiling, pooling in depressions that glowed with sickly light. The air was thick, humid, and tasted of metal.

Tark led them through the upper passages, avoiding the main arteries where drones would be patrolling. Twice they pressed into alcoves as Illithid figures glided past — tall, grey-skinned, their tentacled faces slack with the vacant expression of minds subsumed in the collective. The drones did not react. Fëanon's camouflage held.

But the hive had changed since the acolytes' visit decades ago. The tunnels were alive with activity. Membranes that had been dormant were now pulsing with energy. Repair tissue grew over old fractures in the hull. Cables of organic matter — thick as a man's arm — ran along the corridors, channelling power toward the ship's lower levels.

"It's being repaired," Gundren whispered, running a hand along a fresh growth of hull material. "This ship is being prepared for launch."

Khelon's expression darkened. "Then we don't have much time."

They pressed deeper. The tunnels grew larger, the bioluminescence brighter. Garm detonated a psychic disruption grenade at a junction where three drones had stopped, their tentacles twitching as though sensing something wrong. The crystal sphere burst in a flash of white static and the drones convulsed, their telepathic link scrambled. Tark and Patches dispatched them in seconds — silent, efficient, surgical.

Fëanon flinched. Each disruption to the web cost him — maintaining the camouflage required threading his concentration through the very fabric of the Elder Brain's network, and every disturbance was a jolt to his system. Buumi watched the wizard's face and saw the strain building.

## The Gauntlet

Deeper in the nautiloid, the defences intensified. Acid sprayed from concealed pores in the walls — a biological security system triggered by pressure. Halar caught a burst across his armour and the metal began to smoke. Buumi hauled him back and Cirrus neutralised the acid with a blast of sacred flame.

A corridor contracted around them without warning — the organic walls squeezing inward with crushing force. Garm braced the walls apart with his bare hands, veins standing out on his neck, while the group scrambled through. Tark shadowstepped past the constriction and pulled Gundren free as Gwen forced her mechanical body through behind them.

Further on, a chamber flooded with viscous fluid as they crossed it — rising from the floor in seconds, warm and clinging. Patches spotted the exit and the group waded through chest-deep slime, hauling equipment above their heads. The Mindslayers moved through the filth without complaint, their old training kicking in as though the years between had never happened.

They found a pool of tadpoles — the larval stage of Illithid reproduction — guarded by two drones that had been modified with non-Illithid abilities. One crackled with arcane energy, the other moved with the impossible speed of a monk. Garm, Halar and Khelon hit them simultaneously — a coordinated assault so practised it was almost choreographed. Garm's grenade scrambled their psionic defences, Halar's axe took the spellcaster through the neck, and Khelon put three crossbow bolts into the fast one before it could close the distance. Tark finished it with a flying kick that sent the creature into its own tadpole pool.

The group looked at the Mindslayers with new respect. These men were old. They were retired. And they were terrifying.

## Alura

In a side chamber lined with assimilation pods, Patches found her.

A young woman hung in one of the translucent membranes — human, dark-haired, unconscious, her skin pale and marked with the early signs of Illithid transformation. Neural tendrils had begun growing from the pod into her temples, and her breathing was shallow but steady.

"She's alive," Patches said, cutting the membrane with a precise slash. The woman spilled out, gasping, and Buumi caught her before she hit the floor.

[[Alura]] woke confused and terrified. She could not remember how long she had been in the pod — days, perhaps weeks. She had been taken on the road near the mountains. The last thing she remembered was a flash of light and tentacles wrapping around her head.

Buumi healed the neural damage as best he could while Garm stood guard. Alura was weak but mobile. Khelon wrapped her in a spare cloak and handed her a knife.

"Stay close," he said. "Stay quiet. Do exactly what we say."

She nodded, gripping the knife with both hands.

## The Dome

They reached the Elder Brain's chamber through a service duct that Khelon remembered from the unit's old intelligence reports — a narrow passage used for maintenance of the psychic web's infrastructure. The duct opened onto a ledge high on the chamber wall, giving them a view of the dome below.

It was exactly as the simulation had shown. The cathedral-sized cavern. The web of psionic energy crackling between organic pylons. The cosmos overhead — wheeling stars, shifting nebulae, the suggestion of worlds beyond reach. And at the centre, floating in its web, the Elder Brain.

Nostalgia for Infinity was enormous — thirty feet across, a pulsating mass of grey-pink tissue laced with glowing veins. Its surface rippled with thoughts from a dozen minds, and the psychic pressure radiating from it was like standing next to a furnace. Even with the crystal helmets, several of the group staggered. Cirrus sank to one knee, his chrome eyes flickering.

Dozens of Illithids stood motionless around the chamber, their minds joined to the collective. At the base of the Elder Brain's web, nestled in the psionic lattice, the Eye of Aren pulsed with amber light — visibly brighter than in the simulation, swollen with decades of accumulated psionic energy.

Fëanon stared at the Eye. His concentration wavered and for a terrible moment the camouflage flickered. Below, three Illithids turned their heads toward the ledge. Buumi gripped Fëanon's arm and the wizard steadied himself, pulling the camouflage tight again. The drones turned away.

"I need to get down there," Fëanon whispered.

## The Confrontation

The plan was brutal in its simplicity. The Mindslayers and the group would create a diversion on the far side of the chamber — grenades, bubble devices, everything they had — while Fëanon descended to the web and extracted the Eye. Speed was everything. Once the camouflage dropped, they would have minutes before the entire hive converged.

Garm looked at Halar and Khelon. The three old soldiers clasped forearms. Then they moved.

The diversionary assault was devastating. Garm hurled three psychic disruption grenades into the Illithid ranks simultaneously — the detonations cascading through the dome in overlapping waves of psionic static. Illithids convulsed, their telepathic links severed, tentacles thrashing. Halar charged into the chaos with his axe, bellowing a war cry that echoed off the vaulted ceiling. Khelon dropped to one knee and began putting crossbow bolts into every drone that moved.

Tark shadowstepped from the ledge to the chamber floor, landing in the shadow of a psionic pylon, and began systematically disabling Illithids with precise strikes. Patches descended the wall like a spider and flanked the drones from the opposite side, her obsidian blade leaving trails of moonlight. Cirrus floated above, raining sacred flame and psychic blasts. Buumi stayed with Fëanon, shielding the wizard as he descended toward the web. Gundren mounted Gwen and the mechanical lion leapt from the ledge, cannon blazing.

Fëanon walked toward the Elder Brain.

The camouflage was gone now — the disruption grenades had shattered the psychic web's coherence, and with it his ability to hide. Nostalgia for Infinity turned its vast awareness toward the elf who had escaped it fifty years ago.

The psychic assault hit Fëanon like a wall. He staggered, his staff flaring with protective magic, and pushed forward. The Elder Brain's voice filled his mind — not words but raw intention, ancient and desperate and furious. It showed him the stars. It showed him the loneliness of three thousand years buried in stone. It showed him the promise of the Eye — the power to reach beyond, to call to its kindred across the planes, to finally go home.

Fëanon kept walking.

At the base of the web, the Elder Brain summoned its final defence. The Eye of Aren blazed and from its light emerged a spectral figure — translucent, flickering, bound in chains of psionic energy. [[Theodemir]]. Not the living man, but a psychic echo trapped within the artifact, forced to serve as the Elder Brain's guardian.

The specter raised its hands and arcane energy crackled between them. Behind the spectral mask of servitude, Theodemir's eyes were aware — agonised, pleading.

"Master," Fëanon said. He reached out, not with magic but with his mind — through the shattered remnants of the psychic link that had plagued him for half a century. He found the fragment of himself that Nostalgia had kept — the piece of his magical essence that had been whispering across the void for fifty years — and he called it home.

The fragment answered. It flew back to him like a spark returning to a fire, and when it struck, Fëanon gasped as fifty years of damage began to mend. The whispers stopped. The constant background noise that had haunted every moment of his life since the betrayal went silent.

In the silence, he reached for the Eye.

Theodemir's specter dissolved. The chains of psionic energy shattered. The old wizard's spirit, freed from servitude but still bound within the artifact, radiated a single pulse of gratitude that Fëanon felt in his bones.

Fëanon's fingers closed around the Eye of Aren. He pulled it free from the psychic web.

The Elder Brain screamed.

## The Escape

The scream was not sound — it was a psychic detonation that dropped every person in the chamber to their knees. The dome above cracked. The organic walls of the nautiloid convulsed. Deep beneath the mountain, something enormous began to move.

"It's launching!" Khelon shouted. "THE SHIP IS LAUNCHING!"

The floor lurched. The entire nautiloid shuddered as three thousand years of accumulated stone and earth began to crack and shift around it. Bio-organic engines — dormant since before human civilisation — roared to life, flooding the tunnels with a vibration that rattled teeth and loosened joints.

Nostalgia for Infinity, bereft of the Eye but long prepared for this day, had triggered its escape. The hive was not just a lair — it was a vessel, and the vessel was tearing itself free from the mountain.

"MOVE!" Garm bellowed.

The group ran. The tunnels that had been merely oppressive on the way in were now actively hostile — walls buckling, floors splitting, jets of bioluminescent fluid erupting from ruptured veins. Organic cables snapped and whipped across corridors. The entire structure groaned with the sound of a mountain being ripped apart from below.

Tark led, his truesight cutting through the chaos to find the fastest route upward. Patches ran beside Alura, half-carrying the weakened woman through corridors that pitched and rolled like the deck of a ship in a storm. Buumi shielded Fëanon — the wizard clutching the Eye to his chest, his face streaked with tears, barely aware of his surroundings. Gundren rode Gwen, the mechanical lion's claws finding purchase on the buckling organic floor where human feet would have slipped.

The Mindslayers brought up the rear, laying down covering fire as drones — panicked, their collective mind fractured — attacked in disorganised waves. Garm caught an Illithid by the throat and hurled it through a membrane wall. Halar's axe never stopped moving. Khelon fired bolt after bolt, each one finding its mark.

A section of tunnel collapsed ahead of them. Tark shadowstepped through the debris and found a parallel corridor — one of the nautiloid's secondary arterial passages, still mostly intact. The group squeezed through a gap in the organic wall and sprinted along it as the ship lurched again, harder this time. Through cracks in the hull, they could see rock — the mountain's interior, breaking apart as the nautiloid forced itself upward.

The secondary passage opened onto a large chamber — a cargo hold of some kind, its ceiling twenty feet high and buckling. At the far end, a section of hull had torn away, revealing raw mountainside and a rush of freezing air.

"There!" Cirrus pointed. "We go through there!"

The ship heaved. The floor tilted thirty degrees. Equipment, debris and bodies slid toward the tear in the hull. Outside, the mountain was disintegrating — huge slabs of rock falling away as the nautiloid's bulk forced its way upward through the earth like a creature being born.

Tark grabbed Alura and leapt. Patches followed. Garm threw Gundren — armour, Gwen and all — through the gap and then dove after him. Halar and Khelon went together, the two old soldiers jumping in tandem. Cirrus floated through on a flight spell, catching Buumi's outstretched hand as the paladin launched himself toward the opening.

Fëanon was last. He stood at the edge of the tear, the nautiloid rising around him, the Elder Brain's psychic scream still reverberating through the structure. Through the gap he could see the sky — grey clouds, falling snow, the broken peak of Ladypeak crumbling as the ship tore free.

He jumped.

The fall was forty feet — then sixty — then more as the nautiloid continued to rise. Fëanon cast feather fall instinctively, his descent slowing to a gentle drift. Around him, chunks of mountain tumbled past. Below, the crater left by the nautiloid's departure yawned like an open wound in the earth — a vast, raw cavity of shattered stone and displaced earth, steaming in the winter air.

He landed at the bottom of the crater. The others were already there — bruised, bleeding, but alive. Alura clung to Khelon's arm. The Mindslayers were on their feet, looking up.

Above them, the nautiloid rose. It was immense — a bio-organic vessel the size of a castle, trailing roots of stone and earth, its hull pulsing with bioluminescent light. It hung in the sky for a long moment, as though savouring the air after three millennia underground. Then its engines flared — a burst of psionic energy that lit the cloud cover from within — and Nostalgia for Infinity accelerated upward, punching through the clouds and disappearing into the sky.

The ancient collective had finally gone home.

The group stood in the crater, surrounded by devastation, and watched the sky until the light faded. Fëanon held the Eye of Aren against his chest. Inside it, he could feel the faint pulse of Theodemir's spirit — wounded, weary, but free.

He sank to his knees in the rubble and wept.
