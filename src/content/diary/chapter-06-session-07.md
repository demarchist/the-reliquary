## The Vault of Sages

With the Von Strapps safely away, the group turned to the task that Fëanon had been building toward since the simulation: locating [[Nostalgia for Infinity]]'s hive and recovering the [[Eye of Aren]].

The memory fragments from Lei Tan's reconstruction had provided scattered details — geological features, the sound of underground rivers, the quality of light through certain rock formations — but nothing that amounted to coordinates. Fëanon needed the [[Vault of Sages]].

Silverymoon's great library occupied a sprawling complex near the Lady's College, its underground levels extending far deeper than its modest surface entrance suggested. The main hall was a cathedral of knowledge — vaulted ceilings, galleries rising five stories, and a silence so complete that Tark's footsteps echoed like dropped coins. The air smelled of old paper and preservation magic.

[[Sala Shara]] met them at the restricted archives. The Keeper of the Vault was older now than the young woman from the simulation — grey-streaked hair, reading spectacles on a chain, hands still ink-stained — but the intensity was the same. She recognised Fëanon immediately and her composure cracked for just a moment.

"I heard you were back," she said. "I heard you were free."

Fëanon nodded. Sala studied his face for a long time, then turned and led them into the deep archives without another word.

## The Search

The group spent three days in the Vault. Fëanon and Sala worked side by side, cross-referencing the fragmentary sensory impressions from the simulation against the Vault's extensive cartographic and geological records. The underground rivers Buumi remembered hearing through Sala's memories matched a specific hydrological profile. The rock formations Tark recalled seeing through Elion's eyes corresponded to a particular stratum of metamorphic stone found only in the western Nether Mountains.

Gundren proved invaluable. His Dwarven understanding of subterranean geology allowed him to narrow the search area from hundreds of square miles to a handful of likely sites. He sat with Sala for hours, sketching cross-sections and arguing about water table depths with the enthusiasm of a dwarf who had found someone who understood rock.

[[Magistrate Li|Lei Tan]] confirmed that the drone had been intercepted on the western approach to the city, which aligned with a trajectory from the Nether Mountains.

By the third day, Sala had it. She spread a large map across the reading table and placed her finger on a mountain.

"[[Ladypeak]]," she said. "Western Nether Mountains. There are ruins on the summit — the Lady's Hand Monastery, abandoned two centuries ago. Reports of unusual seismic activity. Local shepherds avoid the area." She paused. "And the hydrology matches perfectly. There is an aquifer beneath the mountain large enough to sustain a colony."

Fëanon stared at the map. "How far?"

"Six days on foot, in winter. Three if you fly."

## The Librarian's Suggestion

As they prepared to leave the Vault, a junior librarian — a young half-elf with ink on his nose — approached Gundren and asked, with the hesitant enthusiasm of someone who had been eavesdropping, whether the group was planning an expedition against Mind Flayers.

Gundren told him to mind his own business. The librarian persisted, explaining that his grandfather had served in a military unit specifically trained for anti-Illithid operations. The unit had been disbanded decades ago, but several veterans still lived in Silverymoon.

"The [[3rd Special Actions Detachment]]," the librarian said. "People called them the Mindslayers."

Fëanon and Sala exchanged a look. Neither of them had heard the name, but the implication was clear — Silverymoon had once considered the Illithid threat serious enough to train a dedicated unit.

"Where do we find them?" Buumi asked.

## The Mindslayers

The address led to a veterans' hall in the military quarter — a squat stone building with a faded unit crest above the door. Inside, three men sat at a corner table playing cards, nursing ales, and arguing about nothing in particular.

[[Garm]] was the first to look up. He was enormous — six and a half feet of scarred, weathered muscle, with a jaw like a shovel and hands that could crush a helmet. His grey hair was cropped to stubble and his nose had been broken so many times it no longer pointed in a consistent direction. He moved with the careful deliberation of a man who knew exactly how much damage he could do.

[[Halar]] sat beside him — equally large but differently built, broader across the shoulders, with a thick neck and arms that strained his shirt. His blond hair was pulled back in a warrior's knot and his face bore the permanent squint of someone who had spent decades staring into darkness. He looked like the kind of man who solved problems by walking through them.

[[Khelon]] was the tallest of the three — lean, angular, with sharp cheekbones and pale eyes that tracked the group with predatory precision. His bearing was the most obviously military, his posture parade-ground straight even while playing cards. A thin scar ran from his left ear to his jaw.

Garm looked at the group, looked at Fëanon's mask, and set down his cards.

"Well," he said, in a voice that sounded like gravel being poured into a bucket. "That's something you don't see every day."

Buumi explained why they were there. The three veterans listened without interruption, their expressions shifting from amusement to interest to something harder. When Buumi mentioned Nostalgia for Infinity, Khelon leaned forward.

"An Elder Brain," Khelon said. "You want to walk into an Elder Brain's hive."

"We want to take something back from it," Fëanon said.

The three Mindslayers looked at each other. Something passed between them — decades of shared experience compressed into a single glance.

Garm turned back to the group. "Son," he said to Buumi, "we have been sitting in this bar for fifteen years waiting for someone to say those words."

Then he looked at Fëanon again — specifically at the Illithid mask — and his expression changed. He stood, crossed the room in two strides, and gripped Fëanon's chin with surprising gentleness, tilting the elf's head to examine the mask and the face beneath it.

"Interrupted enthrallment," Garm said. "I've seen it before — once. The man died screaming." He released Fëanon's chin. "How are you alive?"

"I have spent fifty years asking myself the same question," Fëanon said.

## The Cache

The Mindslayers took them to a warehouse in the military district, padlocked and dusty, its contents mothballed when the unit was disbanded. Garm produced a key from a chain around his neck and opened the door.

Inside was an armoury.

Racks of weapons lined the walls — not conventional arms but specialised anti-psionic equipment developed over decades of anti-Illithid warfare. Garm walked the group through the inventory with the pride of a craftsman displaying his finest work.

Psychic disruption grenades — crystal spheres packed with enchanted minerals that detonated in a burst of psionic static, overloading Illithid telepathy for a radius of thirty feet. Psionic-cancelling bubble devices — small mechanical constructs that projected a field of psychic silence, blocking telepathic communication within a contained area. Crystal-powered helmets — headgear lined with resonant crystal that dampened incoming psionic attacks, reducing their effectiveness by half. Analgesic bombs — alchemical devices that flooded an area with a numbing agent, disrupting the pain-based control mechanisms Illithids used on their thralls.

Halar hefted a psychic disruption grenade with one hand, tossed it in the air and caught it. "These are old," he said. "But they work."

Gundren was immediately fascinated by the engineering. He picked up a helmet, turned it over, and began asking Halar technical questions with the intensity of a dwarf who had found a new obsession. Halar, who had never met an artificer before, was equally fascinated by Gwen.

Tark examined the grenades with professional interest and slipped three into his belt. Patches studied the bubble devices and pocketed two without asking. Cirrus held one of the helmets and felt the crystal resonate against his psionic sensitivity — it hummed at a frequency that made his chrome eyes vibrate.

## The Plan

Over the next two days, the Mindslayers briefed the group on Illithid tactics, biology, and weaknesses. They met each morning in the veterans' hall, where Khelon — the unit's former strategist — laid out everything the 3rd SAD had learned over its years of operation.

Illithid hives were organised around the Elder Brain's psychic web — a network of telepathic connections that allowed the collective to coordinate instantaneously. Drones patrolled the perimeter. Sentries maintained a psychic alarm system. The Elder Brain itself could detect intruders through disturbances in the web. A frontal assault was suicide.

But Fëanon changed the equation.

"His psychic signature is already in the web," Khelon said, pointing at Fëanon. "The Elder Brain has been connected to him for fifty years. If he can maintain his composure, he can mask the rest of you — make you read as part of the background noise."

"Psychic camouflage," Garm said. "We theorised about it. Never had anyone who could do it."

Fëanon considered this. "You want me to concentrate on maintaining a psychic link to an entity that broke my mind fifty years ago."

"Yes," Khelon said. "Can you do it?"

Fëanon looked at the table. Then he looked at the group — at Buumi, Tark, Patches, Cirrus, Gundren. The people who had pulled him out of Barovia, crossed the desert, fought through the mountains.

"Yes," he said.

## Training

The Mindslayers drilled the group for two days on mental discipline techniques — exercises designed to quiet surface thoughts and reduce psychic detectability. Garm led the physical conditioning, working with Tark and Buumi on silent movement through confined spaces. Halar taught Gundren how to use the anti-psionic equipment effectively in combat. Khelon worked with Fëanon on the psychic camouflage technique, pushing the wizard through hours of meditation and concentration exercises.

Patches needed the least instruction. She already moved like a ghost and thought in patterns that were difficult to read. Khelon told her she would have made an excellent Mindslayer. She told him she did not work well with authority. He said that was also a Mindslayer trait.

Cirrus posed a unique problem — his psionic sensitivity made him both an asset and a liability. His ability to detect psychic emanations would be invaluable for navigation, but his own psychic signature blazed like a bonfire. Khelon fitted him with one of the crystal helmets, which dampened his output to a manageable level. Cirrus complained that it muffled his connection to Selûne. Khelon told him the alternative was having his brain eaten.

On the morning of the third day, the group assembled in the veterans' hall with packs loaded and weapons checked. The Mindslayers stood with them — armoured, armed, and looking twenty years younger. Garm had shaved. Halar had polished his axe until it reflected the morning light. Khelon had donned his old unit insignia — a silver fist crushing a tentacle.

"One last mission," Garm said.

"One last mission," the others echoed.

The group departed Silverymoon through the western gate and headed for the Nether Mountains, the Mindslayers falling into formation around them with the unconscious precision of soldiers who had never truly stopped being soldiers.
