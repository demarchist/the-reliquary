## The Gem of the North

The group crossed into Silverymoon proper the following morning, passing through the Sundabar Gate beneath the watchful gaze of two Spellguard sentries who waved them through after a cursory glance. Inside, the city opened up before them in a breathtaking panorama of silver-roofed spires, arching bridges and towering oaks dusted with fresh snow. Elven architecture blended seamlessly with human stonework — flying buttresses of pale marble, colonnaded walkways suspended between towers, and everywhere the soft hum of the Mythal like a song just beneath hearing.

Buumi stopped in the middle of the street, staring. He had not been here in years.

The group wandered the Market Ward, taking in the Winter Solstice preparations. Merchants strung garlands of evergreen and silver ribbon between lampposts while children chased each other through the powdery streets. Fëanon paused at a bookseller's cart, running gloved fingers along leather spines, and bought three volumes without looking at the prices. Cyrrus examined a display of holy symbols at a shrine-stall dedicated to a dozen different gods, frowning at the absence of Selûne. Tark, drawn by the sound of sparring, vanished down a side street and found a small martial academy where two students were practising staff forms in the cold. He watched for several minutes before the instructor noticed him and invited him in. Tark declined — with visible reluctance — and caught up with the others.

Patches lingered at the back of the group, cataloguing exits, blind corners and rooftop access points with the unconscious precision of someone who had been hunted before. Cercei walked beside her father, one hand on his cloak, the other clutching a meat pastry bought from a street vendor. She kept staring at the towers.

## The Von Strapp Estate

Buumi had been quiet since entering the city. That afternoon, he told the group he needed to visit family. His sister Seska had lived here — married to a naval officer named [[Georg von Strapp]], a human. Seska had died several years ago, leaving Georg to raise their seven children alone. Buumi had not visited since the funeral.

The estate sat on a hill in the northern residential district, overlooking the River Rauvin. A handsome stone villa with tall windows and a slate roof, surrounded by bare-branched apple trees and a low wall of dressed stone. The front garden was covered in children's boot prints.

Georg answered the door himself — a tall, broad-shouldered man in his forties with close-cropped grey hair, a straight back and the bearing of someone who had commanded ships. His eyes widened when he saw Buumi. The two men embraced on the threshold, Georg gripping the half-elf's shoulder and holding him at arm's length to look him over. His gaze found the prosthetic arm and he said nothing about it.

"You've brought friends," Georg said, looking past Buumi to the group arranged on his garden path. Tark waved. Gundren was already examining the stonework of the wall.

Georg invited them inside. The house was warm and cluttered — boots by the door, coats on hooks, a half-finished jigsaw puzzle on the hallway table. Children's voices echoed from upstairs. Georg whistled sharply and seven children appeared in various states of dishevelment, ranging from a girl of perhaps sixteen to a boy of four who clung to his oldest sister's hand. They lined up on the stairs and Georg introduced each one with military precision.

Cercei studied the children with fascination. The youngest, seeing the little dragon-girl staring at him, hid behind his sister. Cercei looked up at Buumi, confused. Buumi knelt and whispered that they were shy. Cercei frowned — she was not shy — and marched over to the boy with her hand outstretched. After a moment of terror, the child took it, and within minutes all seven children and Cercei were racing through the house.

Georg served tea in a sitting room that had clearly once been immaculate and was now a battlefield of toy soldiers and scattered sheet music. He asked after Buumi's travels — carefully, the way a military man asks about another's war. Buumi told him enough to convey the weight without the worst details. Georg listened, nodded, and said he was glad Buumi was alive.

The group stayed for dinner. Georg proved to be a surprisingly accomplished cook — years of necessity, he admitted — and the meal was the best they had eaten since Sundabar. Afterwards, Georg played the piano while the oldest girl accompanied on violin. Even Fëanon set down his book to listen.

As they left, Georg pulled Buumi aside. The half-elf returned to the group looking thoughtful. He said nothing about what had been discussed, and nobody pressed him.

## The Conclave

The next morning, Fëanon left the inn early — before the others had woken — and walked to the Lady's College alone.

The campus was a sprawling complex of pale towers, cloistered courtyards and enchanted gardens that bloomed even in winter. Magical light drifted between the buildings in slow orbits, casting shifting patterns on the snow. Fëanon had studied here a lifetime ago, and the architecture had barely changed. He passed through the main gate and the two student sentries straightened instinctively — something about the way he carried himself.

He found the faculty hall and asked for the [[Conclave of Silverymoon|Conclave]] wing. A secretary directed him to the third floor, where a long corridor of offices and seminar rooms housed the city's premier association of practising wizards. Portraits of former Conclave members lined the walls. Fëanon paused before one — a younger version of himself, painted during his tenure. He stared at it for a long moment.

Several old colleagues recognised him. A woman named Tessara embraced him and asked where he had been for the past fifty years. A half-elven enchanter named Delvin shook his hand and told him he looked terrible. Fëanon deflected their questions with practised ease, steering conversations toward the state of the Conclave, the College, and the city's magical defences.

What he learned troubled him. The Conclave's influence had waned in recent decades. Funding from the city had been redirected toward trade infrastructure, and the [[Spellguard]] — once a partner organisation — had grown increasingly autonomous. Worse, several senior members had retired or died, and the younger generation was more interested in applied enchantment and commercial work than theoretical research or civic defence. The old guard — those who remembered what the Conclave had been built to protect against — were a dwindling minority.

Fëanon spent the rest of the morning in the Conclave's library, pulling texts on psychic phenomena and Illithid biology — subjects he had not studied openly in decades. The librarian raised an eyebrow but said nothing.

## The Spellguard Officer

That evening, the group reconvened at the inn. Buumi had spent the day at Georg's estate. Tark had found the martial academy again and spent the afternoon sparring with its students, winning admirers. Cyrrus had discovered a shrine to Selûne tucked into a side street near the Moonbridge and spent hours in communion. Patches and Gundren had gone shopping — Gundren returning with components and Patches with information about the local underworld that she did not elaborate on.

Fëanon retired early and entered his reverie upstairs. The others sat in the common room, talking over drinks, when the inn's front door opened and a young woman stepped inside.

She was Shou — from the far east of Faerûn — small-framed and sharp-featured, with black hair pulled into a tight bun and dark, intelligent eyes that swept the room with practised efficiency. She wore the grey cloak of the [[Spellguard]] over practical travelling clothes, and a short sword hung at her hip. In her arms she carried a leather satchel that she held with the care of someone transporting volatile materials.

She approached the group's table without hesitation. "You are the Spellforged Company," she said. It was not a question. "My name is Lei Tan. I am a junior investigator with the Spellguard's Arcane Crimes Division." She paused, glancing toward the stairs. "I need to speak with the wizard Fëanon Sërindë. Specifically, I need his help."

Buumi asked what kind of help. Lei Tan looked at each of them in turn, as if weighing how much to share. Then she sat down, set the satchel on the table with exaggerated care, and began to explain.

Three months ago, a Spellguard patrol had intercepted an Illithid drone on the outskirts of the city — unusual, as the creatures rarely ventured this close to a Mythal-protected settlement. The drone had been subdued and examined. Among the biological material recovered were several corrupted memory strands — psionic engrams extracted from humanoid brains and woven into the Illithid's neural tissue.

"Memory strands are the Mind Flayers' currency," Lei Tan said. "They consume them for knowledge, for experience, for pleasure. These particular strands were unusual. They were old — decades old — and they bore the psychic fingerprints of trained spellcasters. Conclave-trained spellcasters."

She opened her satchel and removed a sealed glass vial containing what appeared to be a strand of gossamer thread suspended in a pale blue fluid. It pulsed faintly, as though breathing.

"I have identified four fingerprints on these strands. Three belong to current or former members of the Conclave. The fourth belongs to your wizard." She looked at the stairs again. "Fëanon Sërindë's psychic signature is woven into these memories. Whatever the Illithids took from these mages, he was part of it."

Tark leaned forward. "You think Fëanon was working with the Mind Flayers?"

Lei Tan shook her head. "I think several Conclave mages encountered an Illithid colony decades ago and kept it secret. I think memories were exchanged — willingly or not. And I think whatever happened has been covered up ever since." She hesitated. "My superiors believe I am overzealous. They have assigned me a single domesticated Grell and told me to write a report. But I believe this is larger than they understand."

She explained her method. She had developed a technique — the [[Lei Technique]] — using the domesticated Grell's psychic receptors to reconstruct fragmented memories. The process required a living mind attuned to the memories' origin to serve as a simulation arbiter — an anchor point for the reconstruction. Without one, the fragments remained incoherent noise.

"Fëanon's psychic fingerprint is on these strands," she said. "If he agrees to serve as the arbiter — entering a state of deep reverie while the Grell processes the memories — I can reconstruct what happened. The rest of you would each host one strand, experiencing the recovered memories from the perspective of whoever originally lived them."

Patches asked what the risks were.

"Discomfort," Lei Tan said. "Disorientation. The memories are old and corrupted — you will experience gaps, distortions. The Grell is domesticated but not tame. And your wizard will be unconscious throughout." She paused. "But if this works, we will know the truth."

The group exchanged glances. Buumi looked toward the stairs where Fëanon slept. "We'll talk to him in the morning."

## The Agreement

Fëanon took the news with less surprise than the others expected. He studied the memory strand in its vial for a long time, holding it up to the candlelight and watching the faint pulse within. Something moved behind his eyes — recognition, perhaps, or dread.

"I know what this is," he said quietly.

He asked Lei Tan a series of technical questions about the Grell, the reconstruction process, and the limits of her technique. She answered each one precisely, and Fëanon's estimation of the young investigator rose visibly. She was thorough, meticulous, and — he could tell — driven by genuine curiosity rather than ambition.

He agreed. The group would undergo the simulation the following day, at Lei Tan's workspace in the Spellguard offices.

As she left, Lei Tan turned at the door. "Officer Li," she said. "That is what my colleagues call me. In case you need to find me." She nodded once and disappeared into the evening snow.
