The Open Weave Collective is a loose ideological movement that has taken root among the younger students of [[feanons-tower|Fëanon's Tower]] during the wizard's extended absences. Its adherents — identifiable by their deliberately soft, floppy wizard hats, worn in conscious rejection of the traditional stiff, pointed hat of academic wizardry — advocate for what they call an "open" approach to the Weave: the position that sorcerous talent, intuitive casting, and non-traditional magical practice are equally valid paths to arcane understanding, and that the rigid hierarchies of classical wizardry represent an exclusionary framework that privileges institutional study over natural ability.

Fëanon considers this position insidious nonsense. He has used those exact words.

## Philosophy

The Collective's central argument is that the traditional distinction between wizardry and sorcery — between magic earned through disciplined study and magic accessed through innate talent — is not a meaningful taxonomy but a power structure. They contend that centuries of academic gatekeeping have marginalised those with natural arcane aptitude, privileged institutional credentials over demonstrable ability, and perpetuated what their pamphlets describe as a "hierarchical relationship with the Weave" in which a small scholarly elite dictates the terms on which magic may be accessed, studied, and practised.

They advocate for "open casting" — magical practice guided by intuition and personal connection to the Weave rather than formulaic preparation. They hold reading groups on sorcerous theory. They speak earnestly about "dismantling arcane privilege" and "centering lived magical experience." Several have experimented with wild magic under conditions they described as controlled but which would not, by any rigorous standard, merit the term.

The fact that they developed these arguments using the libraries, laboratories, and institutional resources of a seven-hundred-year-old High Elf wizard who built his career on the very principles they reject is an irony they have not addressed.

## The Floppy Hats

The movement's most visible symbol is the deliberate adoption of soft, unstructured wizard hats — wide-brimmed, floppy, often in muted earth tones or undyed fabric — in stark contrast to the traditional stiff, pointed hat worn in formal academic settings. The pointed hat represents structure, hierarchy, and the sharp upward aspiration of classical wizardry. The floppy hat, according to its wearers, communicates openness, accessibility, and a rejection of what they call "performative rigidity."

To Fëanon, who wore a pointed hat in the lecture halls of [[silverymoon|Silverymoon]] and [[leuthilspar|Leuthilspar]] and considers the garment a mark of professional seriousness on par with a scholar's robe or a knight's gorget, the floppy hats are a provocation he is constitutionally unable to ignore and perpetually too busy to address.

## Presence

The Collective has no formal leadership, no charter, and no official meeting schedule — which its members consider a feature rather than a deficiency. Ideas circulate through reading groups, open casting circles, pamphlet distribution, and the kind of earnest late-night discussions that occur when young people with access to powerful magic and insufficient oversight convince themselves they are the first generation to truly understand the world.

They have been known to appear in [[thunder-bluff|Thunder Bluff]] in their floppy hats, distributing literature on arcane equity and engaging townsfolk in conversations about magical accessibility that the townsfolk did not request and do not fully understand. On at least one occasion, a group of them set up an "open casting demonstration" in the market square that produced a minor wild magic surge, singed an awning, and prompted a formal complaint to the barony council. They described the incident as "a learning moment." The council did not use those words.

Within the tower itself, the Collective has at various times established an informal reading room on the fourth floor, hosted guest speakers of sorcerous persuasion, and circulated a petition to add "intuitive arcanism" as a recognised discipline in the tower's curriculum. The petition gathered seventeen signatures. Fëanon learned of its existence three weeks after the fact, during a debriefing with [[theodemir|Theodemir]] that went, by Theodemir's account, "about as well as expected."

## Fëanon's Response

Fëanon's engagement with the Collective is a study in frustrated impotence. He considers their philosophy not merely wrong but actively dangerous — a romanticisation of undisciplined magic that ignores both the catastrophic precedent of the [[netheril|Netherese]] arcanists who believed magic should have no limits and the basic empirical reality that sorcerous power without structured understanding is, in his words, "a fireball in a library."

He has, at various points:

- Drafted a comprehensive philosophical rebuttal of the Collective's positions, which remains half-finished on his desk
- Instructed [[theodemir|Theodemir]] to "handle it" — a delegation Theodemir interpreted loosely enough to accomplish nothing
- Returned from an expedition to discover the fourth-floor reading room, which he disbanded with a single sentence and a look that cleared the corridor
- Scheduled a corrective lecture series on "The Empirical Foundations of Structured Arcanism," only to be called away to the [[underdark|Underdark]] before delivering the first session
- Banned floppy hats from the upper floors, a prohibition the students observe in the letter by removing their hats at the stairwell and replacing them the moment they descend

The students interpret his inability to mount a sustained response as tacit evidence that their arguments are unanswerable. Fëanon interprets their continued existence as evidence that he needs to spend more time at home. Neither interpretation has produced change.

## Historical Parallels

The Collective's philosophy — that magic should be free, intuitive, and unbound by institutional hierarchy — has an uncomfortable historical antecedent. The arcanists of [[netheril|Netheril]] believed in magic without limits, without gatekeeping, without the caution that tradition imposes. Their flying cities fell out of the sky and their civilisation was erased. Fëanon has made this comparison explicitly, repeatedly, and at volume. The students consider it a false equivalence — the rhetorical last resort of an establishment that cannot defend its privileges on their merits. They have said as much in their pamphlets, which Fëanon has read, annotated in red ink, and filed in a drawer he opens when he wants to be angry.
