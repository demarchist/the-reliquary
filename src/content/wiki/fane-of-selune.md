The Fane of Selune is a massive tower of twisting white marble on the mainland shore of Lake Seluna, built by Cirrus Al Kayan as both his residence and the headquarters of the Cult of Selune. It is an architectural marvel that encapsulates Cirrus' love for himself and his devotion to the moon goddess.

## Approach

The Fane's eclipse effect begins before you arrive. As you near the grounds, the bright sun overhead begins to lose its brilliance, obscured by the moon until an unnatural eclipse settles in. The vivid greens and golds of the surrounding meadows fade to muted gray, cloaked in a thin mist rising from the ground. Birdsong disappears. What had been cheerful countryside becomes a heavy silence.

The Fane itself grows larger as you approach, its translucent marble spires glowing faintly from within as if draining the color from the landscape. The meadows encircling it are adorned with pallid blooms and barren trees, their elongated branches reaching skyward. White shapes resolve from the mist into robed cultists standing mute and patient, facing the tower. The grounds are quiet except for the muffled sound of chanting from within. The scent of night-blooming flowers and strange incense drifts through the air.

## Exterior

Outside the Fane are sprawling English-style gardens and well-manicured hedges forming labyrinthine passages. White and silver motifs dominate the grounds, with symbols of Selune — most notably the crescent moon — omnipresent. A massive stone archway at the end of the gravel pathway leads into the grand hall. Through powerful divine magic, the Fane is shrouded in the perpetual umbra of a total solar eclipse, making the tower and its grounds surreal, otherworldly, and subtly unsettling. How the gardens thrive without direct sunlight is unexplained.

## Interior

The interior is simultaneously cavernous and claustrophobic, with deep shadows clinging to every surface. Each step echoes jarringly in the sepulchral hush; the whole temple seems to absorb both light and sound. The effect is of voices straining to be heard just at the edge of perception. A long hallway lined with silver moonlight through elaborate stained glass windows leads to an ornate pair of doors — deep rich wood inlaid with silver in the phases of the moon.

Beyond those doors, the interior is dominated by a massive statue carved in the likeness of Cirrus himself. The statue extends up the core of the tower, housing a hidden elevator system. A platform extending from the statue's "mouth" overlooks the pews and serves as Cirrus' pulpit for delivering sermons. Beyond the statue, the interior is austere, with silvery moon motifs as the dominant aesthetic.

## The Peak

At the top of the Fane is a flat platform open to the sky, where Cirrus can often be heard chanting prayers to Selune during the night. His chanting — which audibly resembles yodeling — is objectively bad, often dissonant or off-key, and usually far too loud.

## The Infirmary

The Fane maintains a functioning infirmary — a cavernous chamber lit by floating orbs of soft, silvery light. Arched stained glass windows filter moonlight into monochrome shadows across rows of beds. Ghostly pearly wax candles hang in censers from the vaulted ceiling on lengths of tarnished chain, their fragrant smoke failing to fully mask the underlying scent of medicinal tonics. The atmosphere is quietly devotional and faintly eerie: a hospital where the presiding deity is the moon and the healers wear white robes.

## Den of Dreams

The basement houses a magical sanctum called the Den of Dreams, open to the public for a fee.

## Administration

Day-to-day operations are overseen by Topsy and Turvy, two were-rat siblings who were among Cirrus' initial converts.
